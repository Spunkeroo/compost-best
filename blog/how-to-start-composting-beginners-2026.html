How to Start Composting for Beginners (2026 Guide)

Last updated: March 2026 · SPUNK LLC
Quick answer: Start composting by choosing a bin or pile location, then alternate layers of "greens" (nitrogen-rich: food scraps, grass clippings) and "browns" (carbon-rich: dry leaves, cardboard, straw) in roughly a 1:2 ratio. Keep the pile moist like a wrung-out sponge, turn it every 1-2 weeks, and you will have usable compost in 2-3 months.

Composting turns kitchen scraps and yard waste into nutrient-rich soil amendment — for free. The average American household throws away 30% of its waste as compostable material. That is food for your garden going straight to the landfill.

This guide covers everything you need to know to start composting today, even if you have never done it before.

What to Compost vs. What Not to Compost

This is the most important thing to get right. The wrong materials will create odors, attract pests, or contaminate your compost.

Compost These (Greens + Browns)

  • Fruit and vegetable scraps
  • Coffee grounds and filters
  • Tea bags (remove staples)
  • Eggshells (crushed)
  • Grass clippings
  • Dry leaves
  • Cardboard (shredded, no glossy coating)
  • Newspaper (shredded)
  • Straw and hay
  • Wood chips and sawdust (untreated wood only)
  • Plant trimmings (disease-free)
  • Dryer lint (from natural fiber clothes)

Never Compost These

  • Meat, fish, bones
  • Dairy products
  • Cooking oils and grease
  • Pet waste (dog, cat)
  • Diseased plants
  • Treated or painted wood
  • Glossy/coated paper
  • Synthetic fabrics
  • Charcoal or coal ash
  • Plants treated with pesticides
  • Invasive weeds with seeds
  • Plastic, glass, metal

Greens vs. Browns Explained

Composting works because of a balance between nitrogen-rich materials (greens) and carbon-rich materials (browns). The microorganisms that break down your compost need both.

Greens (Nitrogen)Browns (Carbon)
Fruit/vegetable scrapsDry leaves
Fresh grass clippingsCardboard (shredded)
Coffee groundsNewspaper (shredded)
Fresh plant trimmingsStraw / hay
Manure (herbivore only)Wood chips / sawdust
SeaweedDried grass / dead plants

The ratio: Aim for roughly 2 parts browns to 1 part greens by volume. Too many greens creates a wet, smelly mess. Too many browns and the pile decomposes very slowly.

Best for beginners: A compost tumbler is the easiest way to start. It is enclosed (no pests), easy to turn (just spin it), and produces compost faster than any other method. A dual-chamber tumbler lets you fill one side while the other finishes — continuous composting with zero downtime.

Step 2: Pick a Location

  • Partial shade. Full sun dries out compost too fast. Full shade slows decomposition. A spot that gets morning sun and afternoon shade is ideal.
  • Good drainage. Place your bin or pile on bare soil, not concrete or asphalt. The soil organisms that migrate up into the pile accelerate decomposition.
  • Convenient access. If it is too far from the kitchen, you will stop adding scraps. Near the back door or garden is ideal.
  • Water access. You will need to moisten the pile periodically, especially in summer.

Step 3: Build Your First Pile

  1. Start with a brown layer. Lay down 4-6 inches of dry leaves, shredded cardboard, or straw at the bottom. This creates airflow and absorbs excess moisture.
  2. Add a green layer. Add 2-3 inches of food scraps, fresh grass clippings, or plant trimmings.
  3. Alternate layers. Continue alternating browns and greens. Each layer should be 2-4 inches thick.
  4. Moisten as you go. Sprinkle water on dry layers. The pile should feel like a wrung-out sponge — damp but not dripping.
  5. Top with browns. Always finish with a brown layer on top. This prevents odors and discourages fruit flies.

Step 4: Maintain Your Compost

  • Turn every 1-2 weeks. Mixing brings oxygen into the pile, which speeds decomposition and prevents anaerobic (smelly) conditions. With a tumbler, just give it 5-10 rotations.
  • Monitor moisture. Squeeze a handful of compost — if a few drops of water come out, it is perfect. Too dry? Add water. Too wet? Add more browns.
  • Check temperature. A healthy hot compost pile reaches 130-160°F in the center. This heat kills weed seeds and pathogens. When the pile cools down, it is time to turn it again.
  • Keep adding material. Add kitchen scraps throughout the week. Bury food scraps under a layer of browns to prevent odors and flies.

How to Know When Compost Is Ready

  • Dark brown or black color
  • Crumbly, soil-like texture
  • Earthy smell (like forest floor)
  • Original materials are no longer recognizable
  • Pile has shrunk to about one-third of its original size
  • Temperature has cooled to ambient and no longer heats up when turned

Frequently Asked Questions

What should beginners not put in compost?

Never compost meat, fish, dairy, oils, pet waste, diseased plants, or treated/painted wood. These attract pests, create odors, or introduce harmful pathogens and chemicals into your compost.

How long does it take to make compost?

A well-managed hot compost pile produces usable compost in 2-3 months. A passive cold compost pile takes 6-12 months. Tumbler composters typically finish in 4-8 weeks with regular turning.

Do I need a compost bin or can I just make a pile?

An open pile works fine if you have space and no HOA restrictions. Bins keep compost contained, look neater, retain heat better for faster decomposition, and deter animals. For small yards, a bin or tumbler is the better choice.

Does composting attract rats and pests?

Only if you add meat, dairy, or cooked food. A properly managed compost pile with only plant material, coffee grounds, and yard waste does not attract rodents. Using an enclosed bin or tumbler adds extra protection.

Can I compost in winter?

Yes. Decomposition slows down in cold temperatures but does not stop completely. Continue adding material through winter — the pile will decompose rapidly once temperatures rise in spring. Insulating the bin with straw bales helps maintain microbial activity.
